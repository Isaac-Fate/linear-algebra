Self-Adjoint and Normal Operators

7.1. Self-Adjoint and Normal Operators#

Proposition 7.1

Let \(V\) be a complex inner product space. If \(T \in \mathcal{L}(V)\) is an operator such that

(7.1)#\[\begin{align}\langle T \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\rangle = 0 \quad\forall\mathbf{v}\in V \end{align}\]

then \(T = 0\), i.e., \(T\) is the zero map.

Note

Note that \(V\) can have infinite dimension. The proof of this proposition is rather constructive.

Proof. To prove \(T\) is the zero map, we want to show

(7.2)#\[\begin{align}\langle T \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\rangle = 0 \quad\forall\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\in V \end{align}\]

One can verify that the following identity holds through brute force calculation of the right-hand side:

\[\begin{split}\begin{multline*}\langle T \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\rangle = \frac{1}{4}\brk{ \langle T (\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w}), \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w} \rangle -\langle T (\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{w}), \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{w} \rangle }\\ + \frac{i}{4}\brk{ \langle T (\mathbf{u} + i\mathbf{w}), \mathbf{u} + i\mathbf{w} \rangle -\langle T (\mathbf{u} - i\mathbf{w}), \mathbf{u} - i \mathbf{w} \rangle }\end{multline*}\end{split}\]

Therefore, we see that (7.2) indeed holds.

It should be emphasized that Proposition 7.1 does not hold in general if \(V\) is over a real field. The following is a typical example.

Example 7.1

Let \(V\) be a \(2\)-dimensional real inner product space. Suppose \(\mathcal{B} = (\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2)\) is an orthonormal basis. Let operator \(T\) be defined by

\[\begin{align*} T \mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{e}_2 \quad\text{and}\quad T \mathbf{e}_2 = -\mathbf{e}_1 \end{align*}\]

For an arbitrary vector \(\mathbf{v} = a\mathbf{e}_1 + b\mathbf{e}_2\), we have

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*}\langle T \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\rangle&= \langle T (a\mathbf{e}_1 + b\mathbf{e}_2), a\mathbf{e}_1 + b \mathbf{e}_2 \rangle\\&= a^2 \langle T \mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_1 \rangle + ab \langle T \mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2 \rangle + ba \langle T \mathbf{e}_2, \mathbf{e}_1 \rangle + b^2 \langle T \mathbf{e}_2, \mathbf{e}_2 \rangle\\&= a^2 \cdot 0 + ab \cdot 1 + ba \cdot(-1) + b^2 \cdot 0 \\&= 0 \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Note that (7.1) is satisfied. But \(T\) is clearly not the zero map.

More specifically, if we take \(V = \R^2\) and let \(\mathcal{B}\) be the standard basis, then the matrix representation of this \(T\) is

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*}[T] = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1\\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\end{align*}\end{split}\]

Geometrically, it rotates a vector in \(\R^2\)\(90\) degrees anticlockwise.

Corollary 7.1

Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional complex inner product space. Then operator \(T\) is self-adjoint if and only if

(7.3)#\[\begin{align}\langle T \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\rangle\in\R\quad\forall\mathbf{v}\in V \end{align}\]

Note

Of course, this corollary does not hold for real inner product space either. Using the same example (Example 7.1), we see that (7.3) is satisfied and yet \(T\) is not self-adjoint since the standard basis \(\mathcal{B}\) is orthonormal and \([T]_\mathcal{B} \neq[T]^\top_\mathcal{B}\).

Proof. For any vector \(\mathbf{v} \in V\), we have

(7.4)#\[\begin{split}\begin{align} 0 = \langle T \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\rangle - \overline{\langle T \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle}&= \langle T \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\rangle - \langle\mathbf{v}, T \mathbf{v}\rangle\nonumber\\&= \langle T^\ast\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\rangle\nonumber\\&= \langle(T - T^\ast) \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\rangle\end{align}\end{split}\]

If (7.3) holds, then (7.4) yields that

\[\begin{align*}\langle(T - T^\ast) \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\rangle = 0 \quad\forall\mathbf{v}\in V \end{align*}\]

It then follows from Proposition 7.1 that \(T - T^\ast\) is the zero map, equivalently, \(T\) is self-adjoint.

Conversely, if \(T\) is self-adjoint, then (7.4) implies (7.3).

We have already seen satisfying (7.1) does not imply that \(T = 0\) in finite-dimensional real inner product space. However, if we further require that \(T\) is self-adjoint, then \(T\) must be the zero map, as stated in the next proposition.

Proposition 7.2

Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional inner product space. If \(T\) is a self-adjoint operator on \(V\) satisfying

\[\begin{align*}\langle T \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}\rangle = 0 \quad\forall\mathbf{v}\in V \end{align*}\]

then \(T = 0\).

Note

Note that we have already proved this proposition in Proposition 7.1 if \(V\) is over a complex field, without even assuming \(V\) is finite-dimensional and \(T\) is self-adjoint. Therefore, this proposition especially contributes to the case where \(V\) is over a real field.

Proof. As we have already noted, we only need to prove this proposition concerning the case where \(V\) is over a real field.

We want to show

(7.5)#\[\begin{align}\langle T \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\rangle = 0 \quad\forall\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\in V \end{align}\]

Consider the inner product \(\langle T(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w}), \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w} \rangle\). We have

(7.6)#\[\begin{split}\begin{multline} 0 = \langle T(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w}), \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w}\rangle = \langle T\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{u}\rangle + \langle T\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\rangle + \langle T\mathbf{w}, \mathbf{u}\rangle + \langle T\mathbf{w}, \mathbf{w}\rangle\\ = \langle T\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\rangle + \langle T\mathbf{w}, \mathbf{u}\rangle\end{multline}\end{split}\]

Since \(T\) is self-adjoint, we have

\[\begin{align*}\langle T \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{u}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{w}, T^\ast\mathbf{u}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{w}, T \mathbf{u}\rangle = \langle T \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\rangle\end{align*}\]

where the last equality follows because we are now considering the real field. Therefore, the right-hand side of (7.6) reduces to \(2 \langle T\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w} \rangle\) and hence (7.5) follows.

Definition 7.1

Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional inner product space. Operator \(T \in \mathcal{L}(V)\) is said to be normal if it commutes with its adjoint. Formally,

\[\begin{align*} T T^\ast = T^\ast T \end{align*}\]

Clearly, a self-adjoint operator is of course normal. However, the converse is not true. See the following exercise (Exercise 7.1) for an example of a normal operator not being self-adjoint.

Exercise 7.1

We have seen that the operator \(T\) defined in Example 7.1 is not self-adjoint. Show that it is however a normal operator.

Solution

Through a few steps of computation, we find that the adjoint \(T^\ast\) is given by

\[\begin{align*} T^\ast\mathbf{e}_1 = -\mathbf{e}_2 \quad\text{and}\quad T^\ast\mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{e}_1 \end{align*}\]

It then follows that

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*}\begin{cases} T T^\ast \mathbf{e}_1 = -T \mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{e}_1 \\ T T^\ast \mathbf{e}_2 = T \mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{e}_2 \end{cases}\quad\text{and}\quad\begin{cases} T^\ast T \mathbf{e}_1 = T^\ast \mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{e}_1 \\ T^\ast T \mathbf{e}_2 = -T^\ast \mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{e}_2 \end{cases}\end{align*}\end{split}\]

Therefore, in this case, \(T T^\ast = T^\ast T\)(and in fact equating the identity), and hence \(T\) is a normal operator.

The following proposition provides a simple characterization of all normal operators.

Proposition 7.3

Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional inner product space. Operator \(T \in \mathcal{L}(V)\) is normal if and only if

\[\begin{align*}\norm{T \mathbf{v}} = \norm{T^\ast \mathbf{v}}\quad\forall\mathbf{v}\in V \end{align*}\]

Proof. For any \(\mathbf{v} \in V\), we have

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*}\norm{T \mathbf{v}}^2 - \norm{T^\ast \mathbf{v}}^2 &= \langle T \mathbf{v}, T \mathbf{v}\rangle - \langle T^\ast\mathbf{v}, T^\ast\mathbf{v}\rangle\\&= \langle\mathbf{v}, T^\ast T \mathbf{v}\rangle - \langle\mathbf{v}, T T^\ast\mathbf{v}\rangle\\&= \langle\mathbf{v}, (T^\ast T - T T^\ast) \mathbf{v}\rangle\end{align*}\end{split}\]

Therefore,

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*}\norm{T \mathbf{v}} = \norm{T^\ast \mathbf{v}}\quad\forall\mathbf{v}\in V &\iff\langle\mathbf{v}, (T^\ast T - T T^\ast) \mathbf{v}\rangle = 0 \quad\forall\mathbf{v}\in V \\&\iff T^\ast T - T T^\ast = 0 \end{align*}\end{split}\]

where the last step follows from Proposition 7.2 and the fact that \(T^\ast T - T T^\ast\) is self-adjoint. This completes the proof.

We have seen in Proposition 6.4 that the set of all eigenvalues of \(T\) are exactly the set of the conjugates of the eigenvalue of \(T^\ast\). But the eigenvectors may be different. However, if we impose that \(T\) is normal, then, as we will see in the following corollary, the eigenvectors of \(T\) and \(T^\ast\) are the same.

Corollary 7.2

Suppose \(T\) is a normal operator on a finite-dimensional inner product space, \(V\) If \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector of \(T\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\), then \(\mathbf{v}\) is also an eigenvalue of \(T^\ast\) with eigenvalue \(\bar{\lambda}\).

Proof. Consider the linear map \(T - \lambda I\). Through straightforward computation, one may verify by definition that \(T - \lambda I\) is also a normal operator since \(T\) is normal. Because \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector of \(T\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\), we have

\[\begin{align*}(T - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\end{align*}\]

It then follows from Proposition 7.3 that

\[\begin{align*}\norm{(T - \lambda I)^\ast \mathbf{v}} = \norm{(T - \lambda I)\mathbf{v}} = 0 \end{align*}\]

Therefore, \((T - \lambda I)^\ast \mathbf{v} = (T^\ast - \bar{\lambda} I) \mathbf{v}\) is a zero vector. This completes the proof.

Corollary 7.3

Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional inner product space. If \(T \in \mathcal{L}(V)\) is a normal operator, then eigenvectors of \(T\) corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal. Formally, suppose \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and \(\mathbf{v}_2\) are eigenvectors of \(T\) corresponding to eigenvalues \(\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_2\), respectively, and \(\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2\). Then \(\langle \mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle = 0\).

Proof. Consider the inner product \(\langle \mathbf{v}_1, T \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle\). We have

(7.7)#\[\begin{align}\langle\mathbf{v}_1, T \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle = \langle\mathbf{v}_1, \lambda_2 \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle = \lambda_2 \langle\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle\end{align}\]

On the other hand, we also have

(7.8)#\[\begin{align}\langle\mathbf{v}_1, T \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle = \langle T^\ast\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle = \langle\bar{\lambda}_1 \mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle = \lambda_1 \langle\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle\end{align}\]

where the second last equality follows from Corollary 7.2. Taking the difference on the sides of (7.7) and (7.8) yields

\[\begin{align*}(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2) \langle\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle = 0 \end{align*}\]

Since \(\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2\), it then follows that \(\langle \mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 \rangle = 0\).

The following proposition lists several useful properties about a normal operator \(T\) if we can find a \(T\)-invariant subspace \(U\).

Proposition 7.4

Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional inner product space, and \(T \in \mathcal{L}(V)\) a normal operator. If subspace \(U\) is invariant under \(T\), then we have the following:

  • ➀ \(U^\perp\) is invariant under \(T\).

  • ➁ \(U\) is invariant under \(T^\ast\).

  • ➂ \((\restr{T}{U})^\ast = \restr{T^\ast}{U}\).

  • ➃ \((\restr{T}{U^\perp})^\ast = \restr{T^\ast}{U^\perp}\).

  • ➄ \(\restr{T}{U}\) is a normal operator on \(U\).

  • ➅ \(\restr{T}{U^\perp}\) is a normal operator on \(U^\perp\).

Proof. We prove each statement in the following. Once the first statement is proved, all the others will follow immediately.

Proof of 1: Let \((\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_m)\) be an orthonormal basis of \(U\). We then apply Corollary 6.3 to extend this to an orthonormal basis for \(V\) by adding vectors \((\mathbf{f}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{f}_n)\). It is clear that \((\mathbf{f}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{f}_n)\) forms an orthonormal basis for \(U^\perp\)(Exercise 6.4). Let

\[\begin{align*}\mathcal{B} = (\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_m, \mathbf{f}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{f}_n) \end{align*}\]

Since \(U\) is invariant under \(T\), the matrix representation \([T]_{\mathcal{B}}\) has the form:

(7.9)#\[\begin{split}\begin{align}[T]_{\mathcal{B}} = \begin{bmatrix} A & B \\ 0 & C \end{bmatrix}\end{align}\end{split}\]

where \(A\) is an \(m\)-by-\(m\) matrix corresponding to the basis of \(U\). (And \(C\) is \(n\)-by-\(n\) of course.) Because \(\mathcal{B}\) is orthonormal, \([T^\ast]_{\mathcal{B}}\) has the form

(7.10)#\[\begin{split}\begin{align}[T^\ast]_{\mathcal{B}} = [T]^H_{\mathcal{B}} = \begin{bmatrix} A^H & 0 \\ B^H & C^H \end{bmatrix}\end{align}\end{split}\]

By referring to (7.9), we note that

(7.11)#\[\begin{align}\sum_{j=1}^m \norm{T \mathbf{e}_j}^2 = \sum_{i,j}\abs{a_{i,j}}^2 \end{align}\]

where \(a_{i,j}\) is the \((i,j)\)-th entry of \(A\). In words, the right-hand side of (7.11) is the sum of squares of modulus of all entries of \(A\).

On the other hand, referring to (7.10), we have

(7.12)#\[\begin{align}\sum_{j=1}^m \norm{T^\ast \mathbf{e}_j}^2 = \sum_{i,j}\abs{a_{i,j}}^2 + \sum_{i,j}\abs{b_{i,j}}^2 \end{align}\]

where \(a_{i,j}\) and \(b_{i,j}\) are entries of \(A\) and \(B\), respectively.

Because \(T\) is a normal operator, by Proposition 7.3, the left-hand sides of (7.11) and (7.12) are equal to each other. Then by equating the right-hand sides of (7.11) and (7.12), we obtain

\[\begin{align*} b_{i,j} = 0 \quad\forall i = 1, \ldots, m \;\forall j = 1, \ldots, n \end{align*}\]

That is, \(B\) is a zero matrix. Therefore,

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*}[T]_{\mathcal{B}} = \begin{bmatrix} A & 0 \\ 0 & C \end{bmatrix}\end{align*}\end{split}\]

and hence \(U^\perp\) is indeed invariant under \(T\).

Proof of 2: Note that \(V = U \oplus U^\perp\). Let \(\mathbf{u} \in U\), we need to show that \(T^\ast \mathbf{u} \in U\). For any \(\mathbf{w} \in U^\perp\), we have

\[\begin{align*}\langle T^\ast\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{u}, T \mathbf{w}\rangle = 0 \end{align*}\]

where the last equality holds because \(T \mathbf{w} \in U^\perp\) for we have already shown \(U^\perp\) is invariant under \(T\). Therefore, \(T^\ast \mathbf{u} \in (U^\perp)^\perp = U\).
